Thai Script

Thai has no spaces between words. Every syllable carries one of 5 tones, decided by consonant class, vowel length, and tone marks together.

🎙 Voice:
  • Thai has no spaces between words within a sentence — spaces are only used between clauses or sentences.
  • Consonant class matters: every consonant is Mid, High, or Low class, and that class combines with the tone mark and syllable type to decide the tone.
  • Live vs. dead syllables: a "live" syllable ends in a long vowel or a sonorant sound (m, n, ng, w, y); a "dead" syllable ends in a short vowel or a stop sound (k, p, t). This distinction changes which tone rule applies.
  • Vowel length changes meaning, not just pronunciation — ขาว (khǎao, "white") and ข้าว (khâao, "rice") differ only in tone/length but mean completely different things.
  • Tone marks only work with the consonant class: the same mark produces a different tone on a Mid, High, or Low class consonant.
  • Vowels can surround the consonant — they may appear before, after, above, or below it (or a combination), which trips up a lot of beginners reading left-to-right out of habit.
  • Consonant clusters are read as one blended sound (e.g. กร, ปล, ตร) rather than two separate syllables.
  • ๆ (mai yamok) tells you to repeat the word before it — very common in casual speech, not just writing.
  • Reduplication softens or emphasizes — Thai speakers often repeat an adjective (สวยๆ, เร็วๆ) to soften a statement or add casual emphasis, similar to how English doubles a word for effect ("quick quick!").
  • Interjections carry real tone and meaning — sounds like เอ๊ะ, อ้าว, and โอ้โห aren't random noises, they're specific, understood reactions the way "huh?" or "whoa" are in English.
  • Two consonants are obsolete (ฃ and ฅ) — you'll see them listed in the alphabet but they're never used in modern writing.
  • There's no question mark in standard Thai — questions are marked with words like ไหม or หรือ at the end of a sentence instead.
